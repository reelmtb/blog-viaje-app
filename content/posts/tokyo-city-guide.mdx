Tokyo is a city of contrasts - ancient temples stand beside futuristic skyscrapers, and quiet gardens offer respite from neon-lit entertainment districts. This guide will help you navigate this incredible metropolis.

## Understanding Tokyo's Layout

Tokyo is divided into 23 special wards, each with its own character. For first-time visitors, focus on these key areas:

### Shinjuku

The bustling heart of modern Tokyo. Here you'll find:
- The world's busiest train station
- Incredible nightlife in Golden Gai
- Peaceful gardens at Shinjuku Gyoen
- Amazing views from the Metropolitan Government Building (free!)

### Shibuya

Youth culture capital with the famous scramble crossing:
- Shibuya 109 for Japanese fashion
- Vibrant nightlife scene
- Street food and trendy cafes
- The loyal Hachiko statue

### Asakusa

Traditional Tokyo at its finest:
- Senso-ji, Tokyo's oldest temple
- Nakamise shopping street for souvenirs
- Traditional ryokan (inns) and restaurants
- Stunning views of Tokyo Skytree

### Harajuku

Where Japanese pop culture comes alive:
- Takeshita Street for unique fashion
- Meiji Shrine for peaceful reflection
- Incredible crepes and street food
- Omotesando for high-end shopping

## Getting Around

Tokyo's public transportation is legendary for its efficiency.

### Essential Tips

1. **Get a Suica or Pasmo card** - Rechargeable cards that work on all trains and buses
2. **Download Google Maps** - Train directions are excellent
3. **Avoid rush hour** - 7:30-9:30 AM and 5:30-8:00 PM
4. **Last trains run around midnight** - Plan accordingly!

### JR Pass Consideration

If you're traveling outside Tokyo (to Kyoto, Osaka, etc.), the JR Pass can save money. For Tokyo-only trips, it's usually not worth it.

## Food Experiences

Tokyo has more Michelin stars than any other city. Here's what to try:

### Must-Try Dishes

- **Ramen**: Each neighborhood has its specialty style
- **Sushi**: From conveyor belt to high-end omakase
- **Tempura**: Light, crispy perfection
- **Tonkatsu**: Breaded pork cutlet
- **Wagyu beef**: Worth the splurge

### Where to Eat

| Price Range | Recommendation |
|------------|----------------|
| Budget | Convenience stores (seriously good!), standing noodle shops |
| Mid-range | Izakayas, department store food halls |
| Splurge | Omakase sushi, kaiseki restaurants |

### Food Hall Tips

Department store basements (B1, B2) called "depachika" offer incredible food courts and takeaway options. Isetan in Shinjuku and Mitsukoshi in Ginza are standouts.

## Cultural Etiquette

Respect local customs for a smoother trip:

- Remove shoes when entering homes, some restaurants, and temples
- Don't eat while walking (find a bench or stand by the shop)
- Keep your voice down on public transport
- Bow slightly when greeting or thanking someone
- Cash is still king - carry yen for small purchases

## Budget Planning

Tokyo can be surprisingly affordable:

- **Accommodation**: Capsule hotels from $30/night, business hotels from $60
- **Food**: Excellent meals from $5-15
- **Transportation**: ~$10-15/day with a day pass
- **Activities**: Many temples and parks are free

## 5-Day Itinerary

### Day 1: Shinjuku & Harajuku
Start at Meiji Shrine, explore Harajuku, end with drinks in Golden Gai.

### Day 2: Asakusa & Ueno
Morning at Senso-ji, afternoon at Ueno Park museums.

### Day 3: Shibuya & Ebisu
Iconic crossing, shopping, evening at Ebisu food halls.

### Day 4: Day Trip to Nikko or Kamakura
Escape the city for temples and nature.

### Day 5: Ginza & Odaiba
Upscale shopping, teamLab Borderless, sunset views.

## Conclusion

Tokyo rewards those who explore beyond the obvious attractions. Get lost in a random neighborhood, try that tiny restaurant with no English menu, and embrace the unexpected. The city's magic lies in its details.

Ready to book? The best times to visit are spring (cherry blossoms in late March-April) and autumn (fall colors in November).
